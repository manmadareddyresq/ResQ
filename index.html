Why Now?

Cities are gridlocked.
Response time is more about routing and coordination, not just number of ambulances.
People expect live visibility.
India adopted ride-hailing at scale ‚Äî but emergency services are still ‚Äúcall and wait‚Äù.

Problem ‚Äî On-ground Reality

In Indian cities, families don‚Äôt have a ‚Äú911-like‚Äù experience. They juggle calls between government 108, private operators, and hospital desks ‚Äî while time is leaking away.

No single, trusted interface to request an ambulance, track it, and coordinate with the hospital.
Dispatchers work on phones + paper, with no AI support to choose the optimal vehicle and route.
Private players operate in silos. Pricing is opaque, verification is weak, and reputation is hyper-local.
Family members become ‚Äúmanual coordinators‚Äù ‚Äî calling drivers, sharing location, updating hospital staff.

India has seen ambulance apps and B2B plays that didn‚Äôt scale. We treat them as risk signals, not competitors.

Solution ‚Äî Unified Emergency Layer

A single interface that handles emergency requests end-to-end: request, triage, routing, tracking and coordination ‚Äî across government and private fleets.

Core Experience

  • One-tap SOS with auto-location & severity questions.
  • Choice of govt 108/102 or verified private ambulance.
  • Live tracking, ETA, and hospital destination lock.
  • Auto-notifications to family/emergency contacts.

Product Modules

  • User app: request, track, share location, history.
  • Driver app: accept, navigate, status updates.
  • Ops console: live map of fleet, SLAs, incidents.
  • APIs: plug into hospitals, call-centres, and state dashboards.

What Early Conversations Told Us

Families want a single app they can trust, not a list of numbers. Tracking + price clarity = peace of mind.
Operators want predictable demand and simple payouts, not random phone calls and manual negotiation.
Hospitals want pre-arrival visibility so they can prep emergency and ICU teams before patient reaches.
