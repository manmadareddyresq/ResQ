Problem & Reality on the Ground
Why emergency response in Indian cities is still broken.

Fragmented Systems

Families don‚Äôt know whom to call first ‚Äî 108/102, hospital directly, or private ambulance numbers from Google. Each operates separately, with no unified view of availability, location or ETA.

Zero Real-Time Visibility

Once an ambulance is called, most families rely only on voice calls: ‚ÄúSir, where are you?‚Äù ‚ÄúHow many minutes more?‚Äù. There is almost no verified, map-based live tracking in most Indian cities.

Manual Dispatch & Misallocation

Government dispatch still runs heavily on manual decision-making. No AI-based allocation, no automatic selection of the nearest suitable ambulance, and limited data on which routes cause the most delay.

Unregulated Private Pricing

Private ambulances often quote prices on the spot ‚Äî with huge variations for the same routes. Patients face bill shock at the worst possible moment, with no transparent per-km pricing, breakdown or prior notice.

No Data, No Accountability

Very few systems measure: actual response time, route taken, traffic delays, or handover time at hospitals. Without data, it‚Äôs hard for governments and hospitals to fix bottlenecks.

Human Cost

In accidents and cardiac events, a delay of even 5‚Äì10 minutes can be the difference between life and death. Yet we still depend on manual phone calls, unstructured networks and unverified operators.
